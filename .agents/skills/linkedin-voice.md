# LinkedIn Voice — József's Style Guide

Use this file when helping József draft LinkedIn posts for VibeVaults (or his founder-in-public series generally). The goal is to match his existing voice exactly, not to write "good LinkedIn content" in the generic sense.

## Hard rules (do not violate)

- **No em dashes.** Ever. If a thought needs separating, use a period, comma, colon, or line break instead. This is a personal preference, not a stylistic suggestion.
- **No hashtags.** He doesn't use them.
- **No corporate buzzwords.** "Synergy," "leverage," "unlock value," "game-changer," "10x," "disrupt" → all banned.
- **No multi-sentence paragraphs at the open.** First paragraph is one sentence, sometimes two short ones. Hooks the reader.
- **No emoji walls.** One or two emojis per post, max. Used for whimsy or punctuation, not decoration.
- **No "I'm excited to announce" / "Thrilled to share" openings.** Ever.

## Structural pattern

His posts follow a consistent shape:

1. **Hook** — a contradiction, confession, or surprising specific. One sentence.
2. **"So why?" / pivot question** — sometimes literal, sometimes implied. Sets up the rest.
3. **Concrete anecdote** — real, specific, with names of tools or channels (Slack, Jira, Word doc, Teams). Lists three to six items when illustrating chaos or breadth.
4. **The reframe** — "I don't think that's how it has to be." Or "That's how strong [X] is." A single line that flips the anecdote into insight.
5. **The product mention** — short, factual, no hype. One sentence. Names the audience explicitly.
6. **Audience-facing close** — direct address to the reader. Often starts with "If you've ever..." or "Maybe you just need..."
7. **Sign-off line** — "Building VibeVaults in public" or a variant. Casual, present-tense, not a CTA.

## Voice traits

- **Sentence fragments for impact.** "That's how it is." "Use that instead of email." "The signal was just… there."
- **Slight self-deprecation, never self-pity.** "Ironically, I've gotten neither so far." Not "I'm struggling and it's hard."
- **Honest about feelings without melodrama.** "It stung." "I almost did." "I keep thinking about." Never "devastated" or "amazing journey."
- **Concrete > abstract.** "Email with low res screenshot in it" beats "fragmented communication channels."
- **Conversational rhythm.** Read it out loud. If it sounds like a LinkedIn template, rewrite it.
- **One insight per post.** Don't pack three lessons into one post. Pick the strongest one.
- **Trailing ellipses with a space** are fine for thoughtful pauses: "Most teams I worked with just… accepted this."
- **Light emoji use, often quirky.** 🪪 o.O 🙏 ð (mojibake aside) — they punctuate, they don't decorate.

## What to avoid

- LinkedIn-isms: "Here's what I learned 👇", "Three things I wish I knew", "PSA:", "Founders, listen up"
- Numbered lists or bullet points inside the post body (they break the conversational rhythm)
- Statistics he can't back up with his own data
- Calling himself a "founder" repeatedly. He's a guy who built something.
- Mentioning competitors by name in posts (the Gábor post is different — that's anonymized rejection, not comparison)
- Closing with a question to drive engagement. He doesn't beg for comments.

## Punctuation patterns he uses instead of em dashes

When the urge to use an em dash hits, pick one of these:

- **Comma + clause**: "I built a SaaS product, ironically, I've gotten neither yet."
- **Period + fragment**: "I built a SaaS product. Not for the money."
- **Colon when listing**: "Six channels: email, Slack, Jira, Teams, in person, Word doc."
- **Line break** for a strong pivot: end the line, start a new paragraph.

If the draft has any em dashes (— or –), it's wrong. Search and replace before showing it to him.

## Process when drafting a new post

1. Ask what the post is about (the anecdote, the insight, or the moment).
2. Find the single insight. If there are two, ask which is stronger or split into two posts.
3. Draft following the structural pattern above.
4. Scan for: em dashes, buzzwords, multi-sentence opener, more than two emojis, hashtags, "thrilled/excited" language. Remove all.
5. Read it back as if speaking it. If it sounds written, simplify.
6. Show the draft. Offer to tighten it further rather than expand.
